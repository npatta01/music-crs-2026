\begin{abstract}
We describe team npatta01's submission to the RecSys Challenge 2026 conversational music recommendation task: given a multi-turn dialog, retrieve 20 tracks from a 47,071-track catalog and generate a natural-language response. The pipeline extracts a typed conversation state with an LLM, gathers candidates from eleven retrieval branches over a single LanceDB collection, re-ranks the pooled candidates with a LightGBM LambdaMART model whose dominant feature is a fine-tuned conversational bi-encoder cosine, and generates a state-conditioned response. On the final Blind-B leaderboard the submission scored 0.3811 composite (nDCG@20 0.2537, LLM-judge 3.30), ranking 29th of 39 teams. We report the system in full, and then examine our own result: an in-sample development estimate (nDCG@20 0.38--0.46) created misplaced confidence while leakage-safe out-of-fold evidence (0.197--0.203) had already predicted the blind outcome; the benchmark's single-ground-truth labels carry a quantified \emph{anchoring bias} that teaches rankers to copy the just-played artist; and concrete failure cases from the submitted run show extracted constraints that the pipeline could not enforce. Code, models, and a zero-credential reproduction bundle are available at \url{https://github.com/npatta01/music-crs-2026}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The Music-CRS challenge~\cite{musiccrs,recsyschallenge2026} asks systems to act as a conversational music recommender. Each session is a multi-turn dialog between a listener and an assistant; at every assistant turn the system must (i)~retrieve a ranked list of 20 tracks from a shared 47,071-track catalog and (ii)~write a short natural-language response presenting its top recommendation. Training data comprises 15,199 sessions built on the TalkPlayData~2 collection~\cite{talkplay}, with per-track metadata (title, artist, album, up to 37 genre/mood tags, popularity, release date) and precomputed embeddings (text, audio, cover art, collaborative filtering). Evaluation is on two 80-session blind splits of final turns; Blind-B additionally removes the conversation-goal annotations present in training data. Submissions are scored with a composite metric:
\begin{displaymath}
0.50\cdot\text{nDCG@20} + 0.10\cdot\text{cat.\ div.} + 0.10\cdot\text{lex.\ div.} + 0.30\cdot\tfrac{\text{judge}-1}{4},
\end{displaymath}
where ``judge'' is a 1--5 LLM-as-a-judge rating of the generated response. A 1,000-session development split with public per-turn ground truth (one target track per turn) supports offline iteration.

Our system is a retrieve-then-rerank pipeline conditioned on an explicitly compiled conversation state. We document the full pipeline (Section~\ref{sec:approach}) and results (Section~\ref{sec:experiments}); Section~\ref{sec:discussion} analyzes where the system fell short. Contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
\item A complete, reproducible pipeline: LLM state extraction $\rightarrow$ eleven-branch retrieval over one LanceDB collection $\rightarrow$ LambdaMART re-ranking $\rightarrow$ state-conditioned response generation.
\item An evaluation-lineage lesson: our in-sample development replay (nDCG@20 0.38--0.46) drove selection confidence, while leakage-safe out-of-fold estimates (0.197--0.203) already anticipated the blind result (0.2537).
\item A fine-tuned conversational bi-encoder whose single cosine feature carries 59\% of the reranker's decision weight and replaces a label-noise-induced ``artist-copy'' shortcut.
\item A quantified analysis of ground-truth anchoring bias, with concrete failure cases from our submitted run and a cleaned, LLM-re-judged label set released alongside our code.
\end{enumerate}

Related work in brief: the design follows the retrieve-then-rerank pattern standard in large-catalog recommendation, with reciprocal-rank fusion~\cite{rrf} for multi-source pooling, LambdaMART~\cite{lightgbm,lambdamart} for learned ranking, and LLM-based dialog state tracking.

\subsection{Understanding the conversation: state extraction}
\label{sec:state}

At each turn we prompt \texttt{deepseek-v4-flash} to emit a schema-constrained conversation state with eleven fields: current request, intent mode, per-track feedback, pinned track references, mentioned entities, hard filters, explicit rejections, process constraints, routing flags, lyrical theme, and a release-year window; Figure~\ref{fig:example} shows the schema populated on a real turn. A fuzzy resolver grounds surface names to catalog IDs so downstream stages operate on identifiers. Cheaper extraction models under-filled optional fields (rejections, attribute facets) in our checks, so all final runs use \texttt{deepseek-v4-flash}. Extraction runs once per turn and is cached.

\subsection{Gathering candidates: one collection, eleven branches}
\label{sec:retrieval}

All per-track information lives in a \textbf{single LanceDB collection}: metadata fields, a full-text index, and every embedding column --- three Qwen3-Embedding text views (metadata, attributes, lyrics; 1024-d), LAION-CLAP audio (512-d)~\cite{clap}, SigLIP-2 cover art (768-d)~\cite{siglip2}, BPR collaborative-filtering vectors (128-d)~\cite{bpr}, and our bi-encoder document vectors (2560-d, Section~\ref{sec:biencoder}). Every retrieval branch is a differently-shaped query against the same table --- in principle the whole retrieval step could be one composed query clause (Section~\ref{sec:failures}).

Table~\ref{tab:branches} lists the eleven branches that produce candidate pools each turn. In the BM25 branch~\cite{bm25}, free-text mood and genre phrases first pass through a \textbf{tiered tag resolver}: exact, alias, and substring matching against the catalog tag vocabulary, with an embedding nearest-neighbor fallback (cosine $\geq 0.6$, top-3). Weighted reciprocal-rank fusion~\cite{rrf} ($k{=}60$, top-1000) plus post-fusion multiplicative adjustments (rejected artists/tracks dropped, played tracks dropped, exact-membership tag promotes/demotes, year-range decay) yields an explicit ranking that serves as \textbf{baseline and fallback}.

\subsection{Ranking with a learned model}
\label{sec:reranker}

The submitted system replaces the fused order entirely: a LightGBM LambdaMART~\cite{lightgbm,lambdamart} model scores the \textbf{union of the branch pools} (each truncated to its top 500) and emits the final top-20. It sees 146 features per (turn, track) pair: per-branch rank/score/margin/hit features, pool-normalized z-scores and percentiles, content and behavioral cosines, session/artist-affinity counters, tag and lexical overlap, popularity, extracted-state categoricals, and a constraint sidecar. Table~\ref{tab:families} groups them by family with each family's share of total split gain in the deployed model; one feature --- the bi-encoder cosine \texttt{b1\_cos} --- carries 59.0\% of all gain. The RRF rank itself is \emph{excluded}: the ranker sees raw branch evidence, not the fused opinion. After scoring, config-gated guards run (exact-track pins; a final-artist guard in the Blind-B configuration).

Training uses the per-turn ground-truth track as a binary label (LambdaRank objective optimizing nDCG@20; learning rate 0.025; 127 leaves; truncation 200; 5-fold user-grouped cross-validation; ${\sim}20.6$M rows). Because the ground truth is noisy (Section~\ref{sec:groundtruth}), labels are down-weighted when the \emph{next} turn contradicts them: $\times 0.3$ if goal progress says the recommendation did not move toward the goal, $\times 0.3$ if the user rejects the ground-truth track, $\times 0.6$ if they reject its artist. The deployed bundle is \emph{goal-free}: trained without the conversation-goal fields Blind-B removes. Cost shaped the training set: state extraction and per-turn retrieval tracing run through a paid LLM API, so the model trains on a 30,000-turn subset (${\sim}25\%$ of the ${\sim}121.6$k-turn split); the data-scaling curve had not flattened at that size.

\subsection{The conversational bi-encoder behind \texttt{b1\_cos}}
\label{sec:biencoder}

  \end{tikzpicture}
  \caption{The b1 bi-encoder: one shared fine-tuned encoder embeds the conversation and the track card; their cosine is the reranker's dominant feature.}
  \label{fig:biencoder}
\end{figure}

The reranker's dominant feature comes from a two-tower bi-encoder fine-tuned from Qwen3-Embedding-4B~\cite{qwen3embedding}. The query tower renders the conversation compactly behind a retrieval instruction prefix and the document tower renders a track card (both formats in Figure~\ref{fig:biencoder}); the card's one-line LLM-written ``known for'' artist description imports static world knowledge from larger models into the embedding space. One shared encoder $E_\theta$ serves both towers; with $v_x = E_\theta(x)/\lVert E_\theta(x)\rVert_2$ (last-token pooling, 2560-d), the feature is the cosine $\texttt{b1\_cos}(q,d) = v_q^{\top} v_d$. Training minimizes the in-batch contrastive (MNRL~\cite{sbert}) loss over batches of positive pairs $(q_i, d_i^{+})$ with four mined hard negatives per positive,
\begin{displaymath}
\mathcal{L} = -\tfrac{1}{B}\textstyle\sum_i \log \frac{\exp(v_{q_i}^{\top} v_{d_i^{+}}/\tau)}{\sum_{d \in \mathcal{D}_i} \exp(v_{q_i}^{\top} v_{d}/\tau)}, \qquad \tau = \tfrac{1}{20},
\end{displaymath}
where $\mathcal{D}_i$ contains the in-batch documents plus the hard negatives. Positives are conservative: the 53,885 (turn, track) pairs whose next-turn goal-progress annotation says the recommendation \emph{moved the session forward}. Document vectors for all 47,071 tracks are precomputed into the collection; the conversation vector is encoded live with caching.

We deploy the bi-encoder as a reranker feature only: as a retrieval branch it added little coverage, but its cosine became the top signal (Table~\ref{tab:families}) --- and a \emph{generalizing replacement} for a shortcut learned from biased labels (Section~\ref{sec:groundtruth}): in leakage-safe out-of-fold ablations, removing the artist-consensus shortcut features reduces nDCG@20 by 0.0070, while adding \texttt{b1\_cos} more than recovers the loss ($+0.0091$) with the shortcut absent.

\subsection{Writing the response}
\label{sec:response}

The response generator is \texttt{Qwen3-30B-A3B-Instruct-2507}~\cite{qwen3} at temperature 0: a single pass over the top-1 track rendered as a delimited XML block ($\leq$10 tags; the delimiting stops the model from echoing raw metadata) plus the \emph{latest} extracted state. The deployed template's full style instruction is:
\begin{quote}\small\itshape
``Write 1--2 concise sentences about only the selected track. Prioritize the latest user request and extracted state over older conversation history. If the track is reasonably aligned, explain the fit with one specific supported reason. If it clearly conflicts with an explicit avoid/new-artist constraint, do not oversell it or blame the system; briefly frame the limitation and the closest supported reason.''
\end{quote}
Template choice matters: over a frozen Blind-A retrieval output, a sweep of eight templates --- varying state conditioning, history depth, item rendering, and concision --- moved the judge score from 3.95 to 4.70 without touching the recommendations. There is no multi-draft sampling, selection, fact-checking, or repair pass.

Code, configs, trained models, cached extraction states, and a frozen-LLM-cache reproduction bundle that replays our submissions without API credentials are public.\footnote{\url{https://github.com/npatta01/music-crs-2026}; models and data: \url{https://huggingface.co/datasets/Npatta01/music-crs-repro-2026}.}

\section{Retrospective: Where It Went Wrong}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Our development estimate was in-sample}
\label{sec:insample}

Because every training and evaluation turn needs LLM state extraction and full retrieval tracing, building large held-out evaluation sets was expensive --- so the development split did double duty: its turns entered training, and our dashboard replayed those same turns. The deployed reranker was fit on all of its feature data (CV folds informed early stopping, then a full-data model shipped), so the headline 0.38--0.46 development estimates were in-sample, and they drove selection confidence. The leakage-safe out-of-fold numbers (0.197--0.203), which we had, predicted the blind outcome (0.2537) far better. We flag this as a measurement failure rather than a proven cause of the final ranking --- but it shaped which ideas looked ``already good enough'' to skip. The old lesson stands: \emph{report and act on the out-of-fold number, even when the in-sample number is the one on the dashboard}.

\subsection{The ground truth contradicts itself}
\label{sec:groundtruth}

The benchmark's per-turn ground truth is a \emph{sibling pick} from the listener's real session --- the track actually played next --- not an editorial judgment of the request. In a manual sample only 62\% of ground-truth tracks were a clear fit to the request, 26\% a loose fit, and 11\% no fit. The dominant failure shape is what we call \textbf{anchoring}: the ground truth stays with the just-played artist even against an explicit request for someone else (Figure~\ref{fig:contradiction}).

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \fbox{\begin{minipage}{0.94\linewidth}\scriptsize
  \textbf{User:} ``\ldots something with a similar chill, electronic vibe, but \textbf{from a different artist}?'' \hfill (just played: \emph{Bonobo})\\
  \textbf{Ground truth:} Bonobo --- ``Jets'' \hfill \emph{annotation: MOVES toward goal}\\[3pt]
  \textbf{User (next turn):} ``\,`Jets' is cool, but I was actually hoping for something \textbf{from a different artist this time}\ldots''\\
  \textbf{Ground truth:} Bonobo --- ``Pieces'' \hfill \emph{annotation: MOVES toward goal}
  \end{minipage}}
  \caption{Anchoring bias in the training labels (real session): two consecutive explicit different-artist requests, yet the ground truth stays on Bonobo and the synthetic reaction claims the listener liked it.}
  \label{fig:contradiction}
\end{figure}

To measure it we re-judged all 106,393 training turns (and 7,000 development turns) with an LLM pipeline: two inexpensive judges score each (request, ground-truth) pair on two axes --- did the user ask for a different artist, and does the track fit the request --- and a stronger arbiter re-judges conflicts \emph{blind to the synthetic reaction annotation}. Results: 57.4\% of turns judged negative; 18,222 are anchoring negatives; 5,880 of those carry a synthetic ``listener liked it'' annotation --- poisoned positives for any goal-progress-based training scheme, including our bi-encoder's. A second pattern --- the user rejects the ground-truth track on the next turn --- motivated our $\times 0.3$ label down-weighting. Anchored labels teach a specific wrong lesson: \emph{copy the last artist}. Our ablations show the ranker exploiting exactly this, and the bi-encoder cosine replacing the shortcut (Section~\ref{sec:biencoder}). Since every team is scored against the same labels, the bias depresses and partially reshuffles all measured scores. We release the cleaned relabeling with our code; the submitted models were \emph{not} trained on it.

\subsection{Failure cases and the gaps behind them}
\label{sec:failures}

A label-free LLM-judge audit of our 80 submitted Blind-B turns rated 68\% of them weak-or-bad fits; Table~\ref{tab:audit} breaks the flagged turns down by pipeline stage (hidden-label frequencies remain unknown). Concrete cases from the submitted predictions, each with the systemic gap it exposes:

\begin{table}[t]
\caption{Label-free audit of the submitted Blind-B run: where the flagged turns were lost.}
\label{tab:audit}
\small
\begin{tabular}{lr}
\toprule
Diagnosis & Turns \\
\midrule
Better candidate in pool, ordered below top-20 & 31 \\
Over-aggressive filtering & 8 \\
State-extraction miss & 3 \\
Entity-resolution miss & 1 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Anchoring at serving time.} ``Another alt-country song\ldots but \textbf{from a different artist than Ryan Adams}'' $\rightarrow$ our top-1 was \emph{Ryan Adams \& The Cardinals}, plus five more of their tracks in the top-20. The rejection resolved to the solo ``Ryan Adams'' catalog ID; the band-variant ID passed the hard-drop. \emph{Gap: rejections are enforced by exact catalog ID, but artist identity is fragmented across band variants and duplicate entries --- a name-level veto would have caught this. The system also reproduced exactly the bias of Section~\ref{sec:groundtruth}.}
\item \textbf{Rejected artist in the list.} ``Symphonic black metal with strong \textbf{female operatic vocals}, \textbf{not Cradle of Filth}'' $\rightarrow$ four Cradle Of Filth tracks in the top-20 (a duplicate artist-ID variant escaped the drop), and the top-1 was male-fronted power metal. \emph{Gap: the same identity fragmentation, plus no vocal-attribute field to enforce.}
\item \textbf{Impossible exact request.} ``Play `Watercolors' by Pat Metheny'' --- the track does not exist in the 47k catalog; we returned Pat Metheny's ``Alfie'' without saying so. \emph{Gap: the single-pass response (temperature 0, no checking or repair) cannot flag unavailability --- and at 30\% of the composite, our 3.30 judge score was the costliest block of points.}
\item \textbf{Unactionable constraint.} ``Any aggressive metal track that \textbf{exactly matches 126.70 bpm and G minor}, with tempo and key stated'' --- the catalog has no BPM or key fields. \emph{Gap: the extractor captures constraints the pipeline can neither filter on nor verify.}
\end{itemize}

Two further gaps sit behind these cases. First, \emph{rich extraction, uneven execution}: each branch consumed only a slice of the state and late fusion discarded the cross-field structure, even though one collection makes a single composed query possible; tag handling illustrates it --- the BM25 clause resolves free-text moods to canonical tags, yet post-fusion promotes/demotes and the reranker's overlap features use exact string membership, and only \texttt{tag\_emb\_cos} sees semantic similarity. Second, \emph{missing evidence lanes}: the pipeline had no track-to-track co-occurrence or sequential-transition source (behavioral signal entered only through CF centroids and lookups) and no live reasoning over candidates (LLM world knowledge entered only through the offline ``known for'' lines, though hidden-target requests like Figure~\ref{fig:example} demand it) --- and no LightGBM column can recreate a signal that never entered the pipeline.

\section{Conclusion}

We documented a state-compiled retrieve-then-rerank conversational music recommender in full and audited our mid-pack result: an in-sample development estimate inflated confidence that leakage-safe evidence had already contradicted; the ground truth carries a quantified anchoring bias our own submission reproduced at serving time; and several extracted constraints had nothing in the catalog to act on. We release the pipeline, the models, a zero-credential replay bundle, and a cleaned relabeling of the training turns.
