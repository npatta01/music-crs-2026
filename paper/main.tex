\begin{abstract}
We describe team npatta01's submission to the RecSys Challenge 2026 conversational music recommendation task: given a multi-turn dialog, retrieve 20 tracks from a 47,071-track catalog and generate a natural-language response. The pipeline extracts a typed conversation state with an LLM, gathers candidates from eleven retrieval branches over a unified track index, re-ranks the pooled candidates with a LambdaMART model whose dominant feature is a fine-tuned conversational bi-encoder cosine, and generates a state-conditioned response. On the final Blind-B leaderboard the submission scored 0.3811 composite (nDCG@20 0.2537, LLM-judge 3.30), ranking 29th of 39 teams. We describe the system, then examine the result. Our development estimates were computed in-sample and overstated performance; leakage-safe estimates tracked the blind outcome far better. We also found many single-ground-truth training labels we disagreed with --- often a label repeating the just-played artist against an explicit request for someone else --- and re-judged them with LLM judges. Failure cases from the submitted run show extracted constraints the pipeline could not enforce. Code, models, and a full reproduction bundle are publicly released.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The Music-CRS challenge~\cite{musiccrs,recsyschallenge2026} asks systems to act as a conversational music recommender. Each session is a multi-turn dialog between a listener and an assistant; at every assistant turn the system must (i)~retrieve a ranked list of 20 tracks from a shared 47,071-track catalog and (ii)~write a short natural-language response presenting its top recommendation. Training data comprises 15,199 sessions built on the TalkPlayData~2 collection~\cite{talkplay}, with per-track metadata (title, artist, album, up to 37 genre/mood tags, popularity, release date) and precomputed embeddings (text, audio, cover art, collaborative filtering). Evaluation is on two 80-session blind splits of final turns; Blind-B additionally removes the conversation-goal annotations present in training data. Submissions are scored with the organizers' composite metric:
\begin{displaymath}
0.50\cdot\text{nDCG@20} + 0.10\cdot\text{cat.\ div.} + 0.10\cdot\text{lex.\ div.} + 0.30\cdot\tfrac{\text{judge}-1}{4},
\end{displaymath}
where ``judge'' is a 1--5 rating of the generated response from the organizers' LLM-based evaluator. In addition to the training sessions, a 1,000-session development split with public per-turn ground truth (one target track per turn) supports offline iteration.

Our system is a retrieve-then-rerank pipeline conditioned on an explicitly compiled conversation state. The deployed reranker is \emph{goal-free}: it uses none of the conversation-goal annotations that Blind-B withholds. This paper documents the pipeline (Section~\ref{sec:approach}, Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline}) and its results (Section~\ref{sec:experiments}), then analyzes the errors behind the final score (Section~\ref{sec:discussion}): in-sample development evaluation (Section~\ref{sec:insample}); training labels we disagreed with and re-judged (Section~\ref{sec:groundtruth}); and the constraint-enforcement gaps behind concrete failure cases (Section~\ref{sec:failures}). The reranker's dominant feature is a fine-tuned conversational bi-encoder cosine (59\% of total split gain), which in ablations substitutes for the artist-copy shortcut the labels otherwise reward. Code, models, and the re-judged labels are released~\cite{repo}.

Related work in brief: the design follows the retrieve-then-rerank pattern standard in large-catalog recommendation, with reciprocal-rank fusion~\cite{rrf} for multi-source pooling, LambdaMART~\cite{lightgbm,lambdamart} for learned ranking, and LLM-based dialog state tracking.

\subsection{State extraction}
\label{sec:state}

At each turn we prompt an LLM (\texttt{deepseek-v4-flash}~\cite{deepseek}) with the full conversation so far (all prior turns plus the user profile) to emit a schema-constrained conversation state. Its eleven fields cover the current request and intent mode, track feedback and pinned references, mentioned entities, hard filters and explicit rejections, process constraints, routing flags, lyrical theme, and a release-year window; Figure~\ref{fig:example} shows the schema populated on a real turn. A fuzzy resolver grounds surface names to catalog IDs so downstream stages operate on identifiers. Cheaper extractors under-filled optional fields such as rejections and facets, so all final runs use it.

\subsection{Candidate retrieval}
\label{sec:retrieval}

All per-track information lives in a \textbf{single LanceDB collection}~\cite{lancedb}: metadata fields, a full-text index, and every embedding column --- three Qwen3-Embedding text views (metadata, attributes, lyrics; 1024-d), LAION-CLAP audio (512-d)~\cite{clap}, SigLIP-2 cover art (768-d)~\cite{siglip2}, BPR collaborative-filtering vectors (128-d)~\cite{bpr}, and our bi-encoder document vectors (2560-d, Section~\ref{sec:biencoder}). Every retrieval branch is a differently-shaped query against the same table --- in principle much of the retrieval step could be expressed as a single composed query (a gap Section~\ref{sec:failures} returns to).

Table~\ref{tab:branches} lists the eleven branches that produce candidate pools each turn. In the BM25 branch~\cite{bm25}, free-text mood and genre phrases first pass through a \textbf{tiered tag resolver}: exact, alias, and substring matching against the catalog tag vocabulary, with an embedding nearest-neighbor fallback (cosine $\geq 0.6$, top-3). Weighted reciprocal-rank fusion~\cite{rrf} ($k{=}60$, top-1000) plus post-fusion multiplicative adjustments (rejected artists/tracks dropped, played tracks dropped, exact-membership tag promotes/demotes, year-range decay) yields an explicit ranking that serves as \textbf{baseline and fallback}.

\subsection{Candidate re-ranking}
\label{sec:reranker}

The submitted system replaces the fused order: a LightGBM LambdaMART~\cite{lightgbm,lambdamart} model scores the \textbf{union of the branch pools} (each truncated to its top 500) and emits the final top-20. It sees 146 features per (turn, track) pair: per-branch rank/score/margin/hit features, pool-normalized z-scores and percentiles, content and behavioral cosines, session/artist-affinity counters, tag and lexical overlap, popularity, extracted-state categoricals, and a constraint sidecar. Table~\ref{tab:families} groups them by family with each family's share of total split gain in the deployed model; one feature --- the bi-encoder cosine \texttt{b1\_cos} --- carries 59.0\% of all gain. The RRF rank itself is \emph{excluded}: the reranker sees raw branch evidence, not the fused opinion. After scoring, two rule-based guards apply: an exact-track pin when the user names a specific track, and a final-artist constraint check.

The deployed model is \emph{goal-free}, trained without the conversation-goal annotations that Blind-B removes. Its training data is the development split only --- the same 8,000 turns used for offline evaluation (${\sim}20.6$M candidate rows): the state extraction that per-turn retrieval tracing depends on runs through a paid LLM API, and we never built features over the ${\sim}121.6$k-turn training split; Section~\ref{sec:insample} discusses the evaluation consequence of this reuse. Training uses the per-turn ground-truth track as a binary label (LambdaRank objective optimizing nDCG@20; learning rate 0.025; 127 leaves; truncation 200; 5-fold user-grouped cross-validation). Because the ground truth is noisy (Section~\ref{sec:groundtruth}), we down-weight a turn's label when the \emph{next} turn contradicts it: $\times 0.3$ if the goal-progress annotation says the recommendation did not move toward the goal, $\times 0.3$ if the user rejects the ground-truth track, $\times 0.6$ if they reject its artist. Two \emph{goal} signals must be kept distinct: the session-level conversation goal is dropped entirely, so no goal feature enters the model (hence goal-free), whereas the per-turn goal-progress annotation is used only to shape training labels --- the weights just described and the bi-encoder positives of Section~\ref{sec:biencoder} --- never as a model input.

\label{sec:biencoder}

  \label{fig:biencoder}

The reranker's dominant feature comes from a two-tower bi-encoder fine-tuned from Qwen3-Embedding-4B~\cite{qwen3embedding}. The query tower renders the conversation compactly behind a retrieval instruction prefix and the document tower renders a track card (both formats in Figure~\ref{fig:biencoder}); the card's one-line LLM-written ``known for'' artist description imports static world knowledge from larger models into the embedding space. One shared encoder $E_\theta$ serves both towers; with $v_x = E_\theta(x)/\lVert E_\theta(x)\rVert_2$ (last-token pooling, 2560-d), the feature is the cosine $\texttt{b1\_cos}(q,d) = v_q^{\top} v_d$. Training minimizes the in-batch contrastive (MNRL~\cite{sbert}) loss over batches of positive pairs $(q_i, d_i^{+})$ with four mined hard negatives per positive,
\begin{displaymath}
\mathcal{L} = -\tfrac{1}{B}\textstyle\sum_i \log \frac{\exp(v_{q_i}^{\top} v_{d_i^{+}}/\tau)}{\sum_{d \in \mathcal{D}_i} \exp(v_{q_i}^{\top} v_{d}/\tau)}, \qquad \tau = \tfrac{1}{20},
\end{displaymath}
where $\mathcal{D}_i$ contains the in-batch documents plus the hard negatives. Positives are conservative: the 53,885 (turn, track) pairs whose next-turn goal-progress annotation says the recommendation \emph{moved the session forward}. Document vectors for all 47,071 tracks are precomputed into the collection; the conversation vector is encoded live with caching.

We deploy the bi-encoder as a reranker feature only: as a retrieval branch it added little coverage, but its cosine became the top signal (Table~\ref{tab:families}) --- and a \emph{generalizing replacement} for the artist-copy shortcut (Section~\ref{sec:groundtruth}): in leakage-safe out-of-fold ablations, removing the artist-copy shortcut features reduces nDCG@20 by 0.0070, while adding \texttt{b1\_cos} more than recovers the loss ($+0.0091$) with the shortcut absent.

\subsection{Response generation}
\label{sec:response}

The response generator is \texttt{Qwen3-30B-A3B-Instruct-2507}~\cite{qwen3} at temperature 0: a single pass over the top-1 track rendered as a delimited XML block ($\leq$10 tags) plus the \emph{latest} extracted state. The deployed template's full style instruction is:
\begin{quote}\small\itshape
``Write 1--2 concise sentences about only the selected track. Prioritize the latest user request and extracted state over older conversation history. If the track is reasonably aligned, explain the fit with one specific supported reason. If it clearly conflicts with an explicit avoid/new-artist constraint, do not oversell it or blame the system; briefly frame the limitation and the closest supported reason.''
\end{quote}
Over a frozen Blind-A retrieval output, a sweep of eight templates (varying state conditioning, history depth, item rendering, and concision) moved our offline judge score from 3.95 to 4.70 without changing the recommendations. There is no multi-draft sampling, selection, checking, or repair pass.

Code, configs, trained models, cached extraction states, and a frozen-LLM-cache reproduction bundle that replays our submissions without API credentials are public.\footnote{\url{https://github.com/npatta01/music-crs-2026}; models and data: \url{https://huggingface.co/datasets/Npatta01/music-crs-repro-2026}.}

\section{Error Analysis}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{In-sample development evaluation}
\label{sec:insample}

Per-turn state extraction and retrieval tracing made held-out evaluation sets expensive, so the development split served both training and evaluation. The deployed reranker was fit on all of its feature data (cross-validation informed early stopping; a full-data model shipped), making the headline development estimates (0.38--0.46 across reranker versions) in-sample --- and those numbers drove selection. The leakage-safe out-of-fold estimates (0.197--0.203) predicted the blind outcome (0.2537) far better; the in-sample replay (0.384) had overstated it. We regard this as a measurement error rather than a demonstrated cause of the final ranking.

\subsection{Label disagreement and relabeling}
\label{sec:groundtruth}

While building training data we found many turns where we disagreed with the label: the labeled target did not seem to fit the user's request. The per-turn ground truth is the track actually played next in the listener's real session, not an editorial judgment of the request, so some mismatch is expected. In a manual sample we judged 62\% of labeled tracks a clear fit to the request, 26\% a loose fit, and 11\% no fit. The pattern we disagreed with most often is what we call \textbf{anchoring}: the label stays with the just-played artist even against an explicit request for someone else (Figure~\ref{fig:contradiction}).

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \fbox{\begin{minipage}{0.94\linewidth}\scriptsize
  \textbf{User:} ``\ldots something with a similar chill, electronic vibe, but \textbf{from a different artist}?'' \hfill (just played: \emph{Bonobo})\\
  \textbf{Ground truth:} Bonobo --- ``Jets'' \hfill \emph{annotation: MOVES toward goal}\\[3pt]
  \textbf{User (next turn):} ``\,`Jets' is cool, but I was actually hoping for something \textbf{from a different artist this time}\ldots''\\
  \textbf{Ground truth:} Bonobo --- ``Pieces'' \hfill \emph{annotation: MOVES toward goal}
  \end{minipage}}
  \caption{The anchoring pattern (real session): two consecutive explicit different-artist requests, yet the label stays on Bonobo and the goal-progress annotation records both turns as moving toward the goal.}
  \label{fig:contradiction}
\end{figure}

To measure the disagreement at scale, two inexpensive LLM judges (Gemma-4-26B and DeepSeek-V4-Flash) scored every training and development turn (113,393 in total) on two axes --- whether the user asked for a different artist, and whether the track fits the request --- with a stronger arbiter (Claude Opus) re-judging every conflict without access to the goal-progress annotation. On the 106,393 training turns, our judges disagreed with 57.4\% of the labels; 18,222 of these are anchoring cases, and in 5,880 of them the goal-progress annotation additionally records the anchored track as moving toward the goal --- pairs we treat as poisoned positives for any goal-progress-based training scheme, including our bi-encoder's. A second pattern, in which the user rejects the labeled track on the next turn, motivated the $\times 0.3$ down-weighting of Section~\ref{sec:reranker}. Training on the labels as-is rewards copying the last artist, and our ablations show the reranker exploiting exactly that shortcut; the bi-encoder cosine is a generalizing replacement for it (Section~\ref{sec:biencoder}). Our judges may themselves be wrong, so we release the full relabeling for independent audit; the submitted models were \emph{not} trained on it.

\subsection{Failure cases}
\label{sec:failures}

A separate label-free audit --- a single LLM judge (DeepSeek-V4-Flash) over the 80 submitted Blind-B turns, unlike the two-judge pipeline of Section~\ref{sec:groundtruth} --- rated 68\% of them weak-or-bad fits; Table~\ref{tab:audit} breaks a subset of the flagged turns down by failure diagnosis (hidden-label frequencies remain unknown). The failures split by pipeline stage --- what retrieval surfaced, and what the response did with it --- and on several turns the response generator's own text flags the problem:

\label{tab:audit}
\small
\begin{tabular}{lr}
\toprule
Diagnosis & Turns \\
\midrule

Over-aggressive filtering & 8 \\
State-extraction miss & 3 \\
Entity-resolution miss & 1 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Retrieval and ranking.} Two failure modes recur. \textbf{(i)~The constraint was under-applied}: asked for ``the Kamelot track from \emph{Silverthorn},'' the system matched the artist but not the album, returning \emph{Fallen Star} from Kamelot's \emph{Haven} (\emph{Silverthorn} is in the catalog); asked to ``explore beyond'' the current artist, it stayed on them (10 of the top 20 in one Ryan Adams session). \textbf{(ii)~Nothing in our data encodes the request}: an exact ``126.70 bpm and G minor'' (no tempo or key field), or ``a song from the \emph{Breaking Bad} soundtrack'' (no film/TV metadata), which no branch can match on.
\item \textbf{Response generation.} The single-pass generator did not properly consider the query --- in particular an exact-track request for a title we do not have. ``\,`Watercolors' by Pat Metheny,'' ``Sarah McLachlan's `Fallen','' and ``\,`Czar Refaeli' by CZARFACE'' were read correctly as exact-track requests, but none is in the 47,071-track catalog; rather than flag the track unavailable, the generator described a different song by the artist as if it were the one asked for (Pat Metheny's ``Alfie''). The response term is 30\% of the composite (our judge score 3.30).
\end{itemize}

The primary limitation is that \textbf{the system does not model a sufficient portion of the user's intent as executable constraints}. The state records what the user wants, but few fields became filters or ranked evidence --- each branch consumed a slice of the state, and tag matching fell back to exact strings outside the BM25 branch --- while no co-occurrence, transition, or live-reasoning lane existed to compensate. Noisy candidate pools therefore reached the reranker, which could not reliably rank the target track within the top-20 --- Table~\ref{tab:audit}'s largest bucket.

\section{Conclusion}

We documented a state-compiled retrieve-then-rerank conversational music recommender and finished 29th of 39. Three gaps in our approach account for the failures we could diagnose:
\begin{enumerate}
\item model selection relied on in-sample development replays, although out-of-fold estimates that predicted the blind score were on hand;
\item the extracted conversation state was rich, but too little of it became executable filters or ranking evidence, so noisy candidate pools reached the reranker;
\item responses were generated in a single unchecked pass that could flag neither unavailable tracks nor unmet constraints, under a judge term worth 30\% of the composite.
\end{enumerate}
Beyond these gaps, we disagreed with many of the training labels and re-judged all 113,393 turns with LLM judges (Section~\ref{sec:groundtruth}). We release the pipeline, the models, a replay bundle, and the re-judged labels.
